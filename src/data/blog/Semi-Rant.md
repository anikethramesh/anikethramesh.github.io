We had these JAM competitions when I was in my undergrad. It was an interesting idea - get people on a panel to discuss or debate any topic. Just A Minute -JAM. A moderator would enforce the 'jam rules'. Anyone on the panel could 'jam' the speaker based by calling out that they didn't follow the jam rules. That would make the speaker forfeit their turn instantly and the person who jammed them would get to speak and so on. 

# My JAM anecdote

The first person to speak for a whole minute would win. The rules could be as simple as 'repetition' and 'deviation' to as outlandish as 'boring' and 'pointless'. Sometimes we used to get creative on the spot make intricate rules like jamming others if they didn't use an alliteration in the second sentence, or if they didn't say 'Heil Moderator' when they started talking. 

I should admit it was a lot of fun being the moderator. Albeit relatively less fun being a participant. Now that significant time has passed since then, I should admit I occasionally 'used' my power VP of college activities to be the moderator for JAM competitions because they were so much fun. Probably should have allowed someone else to do it but what the hell. 

When we were invited to Christ College in Koramangala, Bangalore (Now Bengaluru) for In-Bloom we were really scared because they brought in National level JAM-mers. They infamously hosted jams every weekend and were really seasoned, Mock-UN going snobs with a great command on the rules and sway with the college moderators. Parameshwar or 'Param', the *He's got an American accent so he'll probably win* orator who was was the best of our college, and our hope for 2014. 

As the competition started the moderator went through the rules. The topic- 'virginity is like a balloon. Once prick and it's gone'. Fine, manageable, not the worst I've heard in these competition. It's usually not supposed to be a great topic, just that it's fun and entertaining as a show for the audience. The moderator started running through the rules and we were like 'ok, this seems fine'. 

As we were prepping for applauding the crap out of Param from the audience the moderator dropped this bombshell of a rule - ' personal attacks are allowed'. Our jaws dropped a lot more than the participants. How were we gonna deal with this? Can we shout anything at anyone? Can we do whataboutery? Can we tell people of cuz they're tall short skinny fat crooked teeth fake accent whatever? This was below the belt. But maybe that was the point. To see who can actually tolerate it and not let it affect their JAM. Definitely something that wouldn't have happened post cancel culture became a thing. 

# Its usually futile to try to talk facts and analysis to a people who are enjoying a sense of moral superiority in their ignorance. - Thomas Sowell

Param went up. We were all watching. Confident. He started strong, like he always did. Good cadence, clean structure. He just about warmed up. Then there was a slight pause. That was the mistake. He started thinking about making a coherent point in a game where that wasn't really the objective. Don't quite remember what he wanted to say but it was something about how consent matters. You could see the shift. Just a slight pause. A fraction of hesitation. That was enough. Someone immediately called a deviation. The moderator allowed it. Turn gone.

Then someone hit him with a personal jab. Nothing outrageous. Just enough. A comment on his accent. The exact thing we all joked about internally, now used against him in front of everyone. That was it. He lost his mojo. 

# Today the objective is not to get JAMmed. 

Instead 'how often do you think about the Roman Empire' question, my JAM experience grabbed me in the shower yesterday. Little did I know that this harmless competition would end up being a metaphor for the era that we live in. Just a minute. All that you got. Nobody cares about what you were saying, they just wanted to stop you the moment you falter and say one wrong thing. I think today people are not trying to do the right thing. 

# That’s what a lot of everything feels like now. 

Which is fine, I mean the world doesnt owe it to you to be nice and not pull a fast one on you when it gets the chance. But what I feel is theres different kind of shift. I think the 'system' rewards this today in a way that it didn't earlier. In JAM, it was explicit. The rules encouraged it. Some how in life it’s more implicit because the incentives are similar. 

Speed over depth. Gotchas over substance. Composure over correctness. Demonstrating value over building value. 

# Once you see it this way, a lot of things start to click. 

Now layer AI on top of this. The 'jam' doesnt need to be a person anymore it can be automated. More unfortunately, it can be scaled. 

What happens then? Do humans become more careful? More guarded? Do we reduce ourselves to saying things that are only perfectly defensible or do we stop speaking altogether unless we're absolutely sure. I think all these are happening. Some also swing the other way and become deliberately provocative just to stay ahead of it. Either way, something gets lost. And over time, that becomes the default behaviour. You don’t even realise you’re doing it. You stop listening fully. You start scanning for mistakes. For openings. For moments you can step in. The conversation stops being about what is being said, and starts being about when to interrupt. 

Compassion becomes inefficient. Empathy becomes optional. Letting someone speak becomes a strategic mistake. Warmth, Generosity. The willingness to let someone finish a thought even if you know where they’re going. The instinct to understand before reacting. All this now comes at a premium. Small invite only social circles, high barriers of entry into social circles. Aggresasively selective humanity. I dont mean to sound doomer-y but I think left unchecked we're going to ghetto-ise the world.

Simultaneously, we are seeing a different kind emerging through the natural selection. The brash, brazen, 'cracked engineer', the 'charismatic founder', the 'steve jobs type'. Those who are able to make sense in a way that is un-jammable. Those who can perform in that one condensed hour of coding challenges, or stress tests or technical challenges. Those who can own the room in the 2 minutes that they get. Maybe these people were always valuable, and they just got more valuable now. Actually, not maybe; Definitely! If you can shine brighter in lesser time there's never been a better time for you. However as a student of Michel Foccault I always care about the impact of the stuff that gets filtered out just as much as the stuff that is amplified. 

# Yeah yeah! I get it, the world is a shitshow. So what?

Well, if you've stuck around this long then you obviously found something valuable. However, I think the way I work and interact is very much a conscious approach to solve some of the problems that have cropped up in the world. I think I am in a fringe. There's this very popular idea called evaluating by patterns of failure. The idea is that its easier to spot failures than successes especially in the startup world because the norm is to not become a unicorn. Therefore, just minimise the patterns that make it look like you wont be a unicorn, that gives you a better likelihood of succeeding than the ones who repeat these mistakes. 

I respectfully disagree. I think there's still merit in the old ways. Trying to mentor, nurture, improve and hone people hoping they stay with you. Treating them as people, not attritable resources. Being nice and patient instead of the typical 'silicon valley cracked obsessed dick' founder type. I hear that a lot. That you dont have to like each other, and you may feel like punching each other, but its fine you just have to work with each other. I've been around the 'intense' types. They get things done. No doubt about that. They can compress time, push harder, demand more, and extract output in a way that most people can’t. And in a world where everything is getting optimised for shorter cycles, faster feedback, tighter loops, that kind of intensity naturally gets rewarded.

But I don’t think that’s the only way to build. I think there’s a different kind of compounding that happens when you don’t treat every interaction like a JAM. When you’re not constantly scanning for mistakes, not waiting to interrupt, not trying to outplay the other person in the moment. When you actually let people finish what they’re saying. When you give them space to be slightly wrong, slightly incoherent, slightly unfinished.

Because that’s usually where the interesting stuff is. Not in the perfectly defensible sentence. But in the half-formed idea that needs a bit of back and forth to become something useful. The kind of thing that would never survive a JAM round. And yes, it’s slower. It’s messier. It doesn’t look impressive in the short term. You don’t “win the minute”. But over time, it builds something else - Trust. People are more willing to think out loud with you. They don’t feel like they’re going to get caught out for every small slip. They don’t have to hedge every sentence. And that changes the quality of what gets said. 

You get depth instead of performance. Thats definitely a longer game. Its definitely not for the chicago school of economics, where we have to optimise for a short cycle in a manner thats measurable and derisked enough to get us returns. Its more the austrian school of thought. Or the 'Ted lasso Way' if you want to be more corny about it. The trick is finding a way to raise VC with this approach. Well, its a tough way to sell them. They have their objectives, and return targets and you cant justify building great people or honing great talent when you're trying to go big and grow quickly. Its definitely a space that filters for a certain kind of people. 

Maybe thats fine. If you’re trying to build something that needs to move fast, break things, capture a market, then intensity, sharpness, and that almost ruthless optimisation for output makes sense. However, the point I am making is less about how we convince people we're worth the money, and more about the kind of culture we need to build inside as a team. Because inside a team, if every interaction starts to feel like a JAM, things break in a very different way. People stop thinking out loud. You lose a certain kind of iteration. 

Instead, you get polished thoughts. Safe thoughts. Pre-processed thoughts. Which sound good, but don’t actually move things forward. The irony is that this looks like high performance from the outside. Meetings are sharp. People speak crisply. No one rambles. No one wastes time. Everyone sounds like they know what they’re doing. But under the hood, you’ve traded away something important. You’ve traded away the ability to explore. Because exploration is inherently messy. It requires unfinished sentences, partial ideas, and the willingness to let something run for a bit before deciding if it’s right or wrong. That doesn’t survive in a JAM environment.

Because outside, the world is already a JAM. And with AI, it’s only going to get faster, sharper, and far less forgiving. Everything you say can be picked apart, reframed, or interrupted instantly. At scale. You don’t need to recreate that dynamic inside your own team. If anything, that’s the one place you shouldn’t. Because you don’t build anything meaningful in a minute. And if your team behaves like everything is a minute, you’ll get very good at performing. Just not at building. With the next wave of innovation, we’re in a very different world than we were before. The systems are getting faster, sharper, and far less forgiving. The only thing that doesn’t scale the same way is what makes us human. If anything, that’s the part worth doubling down on. Everything else will get optimised anyway.

And no, this isn’t about being soft. The outside world is competitive enough. You don’t need to recreate that inside your own team. Execution matters. But there is a dangerous misconception that speed and exploration are mutually exclusive. If you are building a tool, sure optimize every second for output. But if you are building something truly visionary, its important to know when the 'move fast and break things' ethos becomes a liability.
